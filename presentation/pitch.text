[Slide 1: A massive industrial furnace vs. a simple grid-world]
"Good afternoon. Most RL environments evaluate agents in discrete, instantaneous steps—like moving a chess piece. 
But the real world is continuous, and it has inertia.

I built the Heat Treatment Digital Twin, an environment designed to push the boundaries of Long-Horizon Planning. 
In aerospace manufacturing, hitting a precise nanoprecipitate radius in a High-Entropy Alloy takes up to 50 hours. 
The core challenge isn't just knowing the right temperature; it’s surviving Thermal Lag. 
If an agent tells a massive industrial furnace to heat up, the air gets hot instantly, but the metal takes hours.

Frontier LLMs fail here. They think in next-token, instantaneous steps. They overheat the furnace, 
the residual heat causes the material to melt or over-coarsen, and the episode ends in catastrophic failure."



[Slide 2: Visual of the ODE math and SMDP Action space ([Action, Duration])]
"To solve this, I completely ripped out standard discrete step math. This environment is powered by a continuous ODE solver—SciPy’s solve_ivp.

It continuously calculates Newton's Law of Cooling coupled with Arrhenius kinetics for both precipitate growth and surface oxidation.

Because the physics are continuous, I upgraded the agent to a Semi-Markov Decision Process (SMDP). 
Instead of forcing the LLM to make a decision every second, it outputs an action and a duration—for example, 
'Aggressive Heat for 120 minutes.' The engine then simulates the differential equations across that exact timespan.

This environment is completely configuration-driven. Drop in a new materials.json for Titanium, 
or a hardware.json for a massive casting, and the thermal inertia completely changes without rewriting a single line of Python."




[Slide 3: DPO Training curve and "Predictive Braking" visual]
"A zero-shot model cannot handle this lag. So, I used the environment to generate a trajectory dataset of successes and catastrophic failures.

I used Unsloth and Hugging Face's TRL to run Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) on an open-source Llama-3-8B model.

The goal was to teach the model Predictive Braking. As you can see in the reward curves, the fine-tuned model successfully 
learned to cut the furnace heat hours before the material reached the target temperature, allowing the thermal momentum 
to coast perfectly into the target zone without overshooting. It learned the physical physics."
